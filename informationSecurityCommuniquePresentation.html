Why this training
Why this matters right now
The current risk landscape
Our recent public announcement has placed every member of staff in a potential information-sharing scenario. Journalists, researchers, competitors and bad actors all use the same window of opportunity. One piece of information shared with the wrong person at the wrong time can cause irreversible harm to the organisation, to individuals and to ongoing legal proceedings.
Who is at risk
Every member of staff regardless of role. Admin, comms, developers, helpdesk, infrastructure and legal teams all handle information that has value to outside parties.
Our strongest protection
A well-informed team. This training is not about creating fear. It is about giving you clear, practical tools so every interaction is handled with confidence.

Module breakdown
All seven modules at a glance

Know your information
Classification and sensitivity
The four tiers of information at CLET: public, internal, confidential and restricted. What each tier means for what you can say, share, store and print.

Verification and identity
A four-step framework for verifying who is making any request before you respond. Covers phone calls, emails, in-person visitors and requests from unfamiliar internal colleagues.

Social engineering tactics
The six core tactics used by social engineers: urgency, authority, social proof, flattery, fear and reciprocity. How they are combined and one universal response for all of them.

How to protect information in your immediate environment. Clear desk and screen policies, secure printing, disposal, visitor management and shoulder surfing awareness.

Digital and comms hygiene
Email, messaging and metadata
Avoiding common digital risks: wrong recipients, forwarding confidential content, messaging app oversharing, screenshot culture and hidden data in everyday files.
6
The insider threat
Internal risks and reporting
Threats do not only come from outside. Accidental oversharing, access misuse, recognising unusual behaviour in colleagues and how to raise a concern without conflict.
7
When to say no
Escalation protocols
The confidence and language to decline an information request you are not authorised to fulfil. Escalation paths, who to contact, how to document and what to do after.

Module 3 spotlight
The six manipulation tactics to know
Social engineers use a repeatable set of psychological techniques. Knowing their names is your first line of defence.
Urgency
Artificial time pressure to stop you thinking clearly. "I need this in ten minutes."
Authority
Impersonates someone powerful to make refusal feel risky. "This is the Director's office."
Social proof
Claims others already complied. "Everyone else on your team confirmed this."
Flattery
Warmth and compliments to lower your guard. "You have always been so helpful."
Fear
Threatens negative outcomes for non-compliance. "You will be asked why you refused."
Reciprocity
Does something small first, then calls in a favour. "I sent you that report last month."
The universal response
Whatever tactic is used, your response is the same. Acknowledge, pause, tell them you will follow up, end the interaction and consult your manager before taking any action.

Module 2 spotlight
The four-step verification framework
Use this framework every time someone requests information, access or action on behalf of an external or unfamiliar party.
1
Pause
Do not respond to the request immediately. Acknowledge you heard them, then pause before taking any action whatsoever.
2
Question
Ask for their full name, organisation, role and a direct contact number or official email address. Write it down.
3
Verify independently
Look up the organisation through official sources. Call back on a number you find yourself, never one they gave you.
4
Escalate if unsure
If you cannot verify, do not share. Tell them you will follow up and immediately notify your line manager or security team.

Staff expectations
What is required from every member of staff
1
All seven modules and the final assessment must be completed within the timelines communicated by your line manager.
2
A minimum score of 3 out of 5 is required to pass each module quiz. Failed modules can be retaken after reviewing the core knowledge section.

Questions and support
Contact your line manager or the designated Information Security lead at any time. Do not attempt to handle sensitive requests alone if you are uncertain.

The one principle to carry with you
Before the training begins
If you are ever unsure whether to share information with someone, the answer is always to pause and check before you act. Legitimate requests can always wait for a proper verification process. Pressure to act quickly, without verification, is itself a warning sign. You have both the right and the responsibility to slow down.
